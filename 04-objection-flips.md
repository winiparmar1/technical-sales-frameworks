# Objection Flips — Reframe Instead of Defend

> The worst thing you can do when you hear an objection is answer it directly. Reframe it instead.

---

## "We already have tools in place for this."

**The flip:**
> "That makes total sense — most teams at your scale do. The question I'd ask is: are those tools talking to each other, or are you managing the gaps between them? Usually that's where the real cost lives."

*Move: from "we don't need you" → "tell me about the gaps between your tools"*

---

## "Your pricing is too high compared to what we're paying now."

**The flip:**
> "Let's pressure test that. What's your current fully-loaded cost — tool licenses plus the engineering hours spent maintaining and stitching them together? Most teams we work with find they're spending 40% more than the line item."

*Move: from price comparison → total cost of ownership*

---

## "We're not ready to make a decision right now."

**The flip:**
> "What would have to be true for you to be ready? And is there a cost to waiting — a release cycle, a headcount freeze, a board commitment — that makes this quarter different from next?"

*Move: from timeline deflection → surface the real blocker*

---

## "We could build this internally."

**The flip:**
> "You absolutely could — your team is probably capable of it. The real question is whether that's the highest-value use of their time. What would they not be building while they're building this?"

*Move: from build vs buy → opportunity cost of engineering time*

---

## "We need to see how the market shakes out first."

**The flip:**
> "That's a reasonable instinct. But the companies I've seen win in your space aren't waiting — they're moving now and using the consolidation period to lock in advantages. What's your read on what your competitors are doing?"

*Move: from wait-and-see → competitive urgency*
