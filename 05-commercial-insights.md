# Commercial Insights — What I've Learned Closing Enterprise Infra and SaaS

> 11 years. BrowserStack → LambdaTest → Katalon. T-Mobile, Berkshire Hathaway, Amazon, eBay, Bell Canada. Here's what's actually true.

---

## Cloud infra and SaaS are the same deal dressed differently

Both require multi-threading. Both have a technical buyer who can kill you and an economic buyer who can save you. The difference is that infra buyers are skeptics by default — they've been burned by vendor promises before. Your first job is to stop selling and start diagnosing.

---

## The person who introduces you is not always your champion

In enterprise deals, the warm intro often comes from someone with no budget authority and no internal influence. Map the org before you trust the intro.

Ask early: *"Who owns the budget line for tooling and infrastructure?"* That person is your real target.

---

## Status quo is always your biggest competitor

Not the other vendor in the eval. Not the build-it-ourselves threat. It's the decision to do nothing — because doing nothing feels safe.

Your job is to make the cost of inaction more visible than the risk of change. Use their own numbers to do it.

---

## Multi-year deals are won in the first conversation

The buyer who commits to 3 years upfront ($850K TCV, in my experience) is not doing it because of a discount. They're doing it because they trust that you've understood their business better than anyone else in the eval.

That trust is built in discovery — not in the commercial conversation.

---

## The Canadian market taught me patience. US enterprise taught me urgency.

Managing Bell Canada alongside T-Mobile showed me that deal cadence is cultural. The best reps adapt their pace without losing control of the process.

Patience is not passivity. It's knowing when to push and when to let silence work.

---

## The number that unlocks everything

Most buyers have never calculated what a release delay or a QA failure actually costs in dollars. When you help them build that number — even roughly — two things happen:

1. They feel understood in a way most reps never achieve
2. The ROI conversation becomes trivial, because they did the math themselves

Get them to say the number. Then be quiet.
